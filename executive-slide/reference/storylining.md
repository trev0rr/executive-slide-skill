# Storylining — Pyramid Principle, MECE, and Deck Structure

Read this before outlining any deck. The storyline is decided **before** any slide is designed. A deck with beautiful slides and a weak storyline fails; the reverse rarely does.

## 1. The Pyramid Principle

Structure every deck top-down in four levels:

| Level | Question it answers | Where it lives |
|---|---|---|
| **Tagline** | "If you had 15 seconds, how would you state your recommendation?" | Title slide subtitle and the opening sentence of the executive summary |
| **Executive summary** | "If they only read one page, what's the key message?" | One slide: the governing thought + the 2–4 supporting arguments |
| **Pack** | "What's the proof?" | The body slides — one argument or evidence block per slide, told with charts and visuals |
| **Appendix** | "Where's the backup?" | Supporting data, calculations, methodology. Never walked through |

**Conclusion first, then arguments.** State the recommendation before the evidence, on the deck level and on every slide. The audience hears the answer, then decides whether the support convinces them.

- Good: "Our analysis shows a 15% ROI."
- Bad: "We ran 10 regression models…" (narrating your journey — the audience does not care how hard you worked)

Cut the deck to the minimum needed to tell the story. Everything else moves to the appendix, not onto crowded slides.

## 2. MECE issue trees

Before writing slides, build the logic tree:

1. **Governing question** — the single question the deck answers (e.g., "How much growth can RSG generate from entering the private moving market?").
2. **Governing thought** — the one-sentence answer (e.g., "RSG has an opportunity to generate an incremental $4.6B in high-margin revenue").
3. **2–4 MECE branches** — mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive arguments that together prove the governing thought (e.g., "The market is highly fragmented" / "RSG can win on price" / "The residential moving market is very large").
4. **Evidence leaves** — the facts under each branch (e.g., "No major players in long distance"; "RSG has built-in economies of scale"; "33M annual moves"; "$50B market").

Mapping tree → deck: the governing thought is the executive summary; each branch becomes one body slide (or one section in longer decks); the leaves become that slide's evidence — its chart data and supporting points. If a leaf doesn't support a branch, it goes to the appendix or gets cut.

MECE check: branches must not overlap (no fact belongs under two branches) and must leave no obvious counter-question unanswered ("what about capital requirements?" should have a home).

## 3. Deck construction workflow

The professional sequence — adapt it as the mandatory outline step:

1. **Structure diagram** — determine the main message and the MECE tree (section 2).
2. **Storyboard** — sketch each slide's main point as a one-line action title. 4–6 panels per page when sketching by hand; for generation, this is the ordered action-title list.
3. **Ghost deck** — convert panels to pages: each page gets its headline and a note of what visual will prove it, before any polish.
4. **Draft iterations** — build and refine. Better done than right: a complete rough draft beats three perfect slides.

## 4. The headline-flow test

After drafting the action-title list, read **only the titles, in order**. They must:

- Flow logically — each follows from the last, building toward the conclusion
- Tell the complete story — a reader who sees nothing but headlines gets the full argument
- Make a point — every title is a claim, never a topic label
- Stay relevant and interesting to this audience
- Use active language and keywords; no unnecessary words

If the titles alone don't tell the story, fix the storyline before designing a single slide.

## 5. Three standard deck types

| Type | When used | Characteristics | Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Summary** (~30-min discussion) | Executive/board updates; informal check-ins | Highlights key insights; limited analysis; crisp visuals | Pages must be self-explanatory; each page can stand alone |
| **Deep dive** (~90-min presentation) | Project reviews; proposals | Broad analysis; strong logic structure; variable length | Start with key messages; push detail to appendix |
| **Backup** (never walked through) | Reference material | Data and full analysis; appendix-style layout | Include an index; logical flow less critical |

Default to **Summary** unless told otherwise.

## 6. Persuasive vs. informative

| | Persuasive | Informative |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Change minds | Share knowledge |
| Tone | Motivational, urgent | Clear, objective |
| Ending | Call to action — the specific decision or next step requested | Takeaway clarity — the one thing to remember |

Pick one before outlining; it determines the closing slide and the tone of every title.

## 7. Stand-alone rule

Build every deck as a stand-alone deliverable that is clear **without narration**. Decks get forwarded, re-used, and read cold by people who never saw the presentation. Consequences:

- Every claim is sourced on the slide where it appears
- No slide depends on a verbal explanation to make sense
- Labels, units, and definitions appear on the slide, not in the speaker's head

## 8. Automatic deck structure

For any deck with **4 or more content slides**, prepend:

1. **Title slide** — deck title (the tagline works well), client/context name, date. Minimal.
2. **Executive summary slide** — governing thought as the action title; the MECE branches as the supporting arguments, each in one line, in the order the deck will prove them.

Shorter outputs (1–3 slides) skip both. A persuasive deck also ends with a next-steps/ask slide; an informative deck ends on the strongest takeaway.

## 9. Volume rules of thumb

Apply per slide when writing the storyboard:

- **1-6-6**: one main idea; at most 6 supporting points; at most ~6–8 words per point
- **5/5/5**: no more than ~5 words per line of display text, ~5 lines of text per block, and never 5 text-heavy slides in a row
- **2.4.8**: plan ~2 minutes of discussion per slide — if a slide can't carry 2 minutes, merge it; if it needs 5, split it
- **10/20/30 spirit**: fewer slides at higher abstraction beats many thin slides; detail lives in the appendix, never in smaller fonts
